24/7 Emergency Veterinary Care

Rapid response for critical situations. Our mobile units are fully equipped for urgent equine and livestock care.

Emergency Call Procedures

  1. Call the Emergency Line Immediately

    Dial 1-800-VET-FARM. If after hours, our answering service will immediately page the on-call veterinarian who will call you back within 15 minutes.

  2. Assess & Report the Situation

    Provide the dispatcher or veterinarian with the exact location (stable address or farm name), animal type, and primary symptoms (e.g., colic signs, severe laceration, dystocia).

  3. Follow Vet Instructions

    Keep the animal as calm and safe as possible. Do not administer any medications (like Banamine) unless specifically instructed by the veterinarian over the phone, as this can mask critical diagnostic symptoms.

Common Emergencies We Treat

Urgent Equine Care & Colic

Colic requires immediate attention. We provide on-site medical management of colic, including physical exams, rectal palpation, nasogastric intubation, and IV fluid therapy. We also handle acute laminitis, choke, and tying up.

Stable Emergencies & Trauma

Rapid response for severe lacerations, puncture wounds, eye injuries, and acute severe lameness (suspected fractures or infections). Our trucks are equipped with portable digital radiography and ultrasound.

Livestock Dystocia

Emergency assistance for difficult births (calving, foaling, lambing, kidding). Immediate intervention is critical for the survival of both the mother and the neonate.

Is It an Emergency? Triage Checker

Determine whether your animal requires immediate after-hours paging or can be scheduled during normal business routes.

🚨 Call Immediately (24/7)

Severe / Progressive Colic

Violent rolling, sweating, biting at flanks, inability to stand, or elevated heart rates.

Severe Trauma or Fractures

Non-weight-bearing lameness, exposed bone, deep arterial bleeding, or lacerations.

Dystocia (Foaling / Calving Difficulties)

No progress within 20 minutes of active straining, abnormal presentation of legs/nose.

Acute Ocular (Eye) Injury

Squinting, swelling, cloudiness, pupil dilation, or active puncture of the eye structure.

🟢 Schedule Routine Call (Weekday)

Mild Hives or Skin Swelling

Itchy bumps, stable breathing, normal appetite, and normal rectal temperature.

Minor Scraping / Abrasions

Surface cuts that do not expose muscle/bone, minimal bleeding, and normal movement.

Chronic Mild Lameness

Slight head-bobbing at trot, no swelling/heat in limbs, and sound at the walk.

Lost Shoe / Minor Hoof Issues

Unless accompanied by acute heat, pulsing, or absolute refusal to bear weight.

Preparing for the Vet's Arrival

During an emergency, every second counts. Taking these pre-arrival preparation steps makes treatment much safer and faster for our veterinary team.

Contact Dispatch

Halter and Confine

Put a halter and lead rope on the animal. Secure them in a dry, clean box stall, dry pen, or squeeze chute. Avoid letting colicky horses roll violently.

Secure Light & Shelter

If the emergency occurs at night, ensure a clean, covered workspace is available with bright lighting and access to fresh warm water.

Clear Ambulation Pathways

Open gates, move farm machinery, and clear driveways so the vet's mobile truck can park directly next to the barn door.

Emergency Care FAQs

Answers to common logistical questions regarding clinical emergencies and on-farm surgical setups.

How quickly will the veterinarian arrive at my property?
+

Our average emergency dispatch travel time is under 45 minutes, depending on your farm location and current route status. The paging doctor will give you an exact estimated arrival time (ETA) when calling you back.

Should I administer pain medication (like Banamine) before the vet arrives?
+

No. Please do not give any NSAIDs, pain killers, or sedatives unless explicitly directed by the on-call veterinarian. Administering drugs beforehand can mask symptoms, alter vital signs, and complicate colic or lameness diagnoses.

What happens if my horse needs surgical hospitalization?
+

For critical cases that require intensive inpatient surgical intervention (such as surgical colic or fracture repair), our veterinarians will stabilize the animal on-site, administer initial support therapies, and coordinate immediate referral and records transfer to the nearest regional veterinary hospital.
